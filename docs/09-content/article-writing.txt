Document Description
--------------------

This document defines Massage Nexus article-writing guidance, article topic scope, title selection, target article length ranges, Neural Agent use in article planning, approved article categories, target audience labels, research and source handling, visual direction, article NSFW levels, reading-duration handling, downloadable text-file output rules, article rating/reaction references, reputation-effect references, reward references, data-structure references, reusable article-thread and follow-up prompts, and the working article topic calendar.

The listed calendar dates are brainstorming and planning guides for release order and seasonal timing, not permanent publication records. Once an article is published, remove it from this working list or move it to a separate published-content tracker.

Core Article Direction
----------------------

Massage Nexus articles should help readers discover, understand, compare, discuss, and experience massage, spa, and wellness services with more confidence. Articles may cover practical spa-going guidance, massage education, practitioner and spa professionalism, local and global spa culture, wellness habits, caregiving, products, business operations, public awareness, and light fictional or comic-adjacent topics when they still connect to rest, touch, service culture, bodywork, or wellness discovery.

Topic scope is about editorial focus, not censorship. Official Massage Nexus articles should avoid unrelated subjects unless there is a clear connection to the spa or wellness experience; for example, car repair is outside article scope by itself, but parking access, commuting to a spa, rider delivery interruptions, accessibility, or transport around a spa may be relevant if the article clearly connects those details back to the user experience.

Target Article Length
---------------------

- Normal published article target: 900 to 1,500 words, roughly 6,000 to 11,000 characters.
- Minimum normal article: 700 words, roughly 4,000 to 5,000 characters.
- Maximum normal article: 2,000 words, roughly 12,000 to 15,000 characters.
- Deep guide, professional guide, medical-caution guide, business guide, or complex safety guide: 1,800 to 3,000 words, roughly 12,000 to 22,500 characters.
- Hard upper limit before splitting into multiple articles: 4,000 words, roughly 24,000 to 30,000 characters.
- Short focused Article: 300 to 700 words, roughly 1,800 to 5,000 characters.

These ranges are targets, not prison bars. A short article is acceptable when the topic is simple and complete, while a longer article is acceptable when the subject needs explanation, safety notes, examples, comparison, or step-by-step guidance.

Reading Duration Guidance
-------------------------

Article reading duration should be computed systematically and stored internally in seconds.

Display preference:

- show minutes and seconds;
- do not display only rounded minutes;
- round up when the computed value has decimals or milliseconds.

Examples:

- 45 seconds;
- 1 minute 30 seconds;
- 3 minutes 12 seconds.

Reading Duration Types
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Massage Nexus should support two reading-duration estimates:

- visual reading duration;
- spoken reading duration.

Visual reading duration estimates silent reading.

Spoken reading duration estimates reading aloud, screen-reader playback, or text-to-speech listening at a normal pace.

Current Article structures store reading_duration_visual and reading_duration_spoken separately in seconds. article_body also stores the system-generated visible word_count and article_plain_text used to calculate the estimates and support search without indexing HTML class names or markup.

Working Formula for Latin-Script Articles
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

For English, Filipino, Taglish, Spanish, Bisaya, and similar space-separated languages:

Code block (text):
    visual_reading_seconds = ceil((word_count / 225) * 60)
    spoken_reading_seconds = ceil((word_count / 150) * 60)

The divisor represents estimated words per minute.

For dense technical, legal, medical, policy, professional, or heavily instructional content, the visual reading divisor may be slower:

Code block (text):
    visual_reading_seconds = ceil((word_count / 200) * 60)

This dense-content adjustment is more likely to apply to articles, policies, legal pages, safety guides, medical-caution articles, and professional guides than to normal narrative reviews.

Character-Based Fallback
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

For Korean, Japanese, Chinese, and other languages where word counting may be unreliable, a future calibrated reading-duration system should be created.

Temporary fallback candidates:

Code block (text):
    visual_reading_seconds = ceil((character_count / 900) * 60)
    spoken_reading_seconds = ceil((character_count / 650) * 60)

These fallback values are not final linguistic standards. Korean calibration should be prioritized before Japanese and Chinese if Korean support arrives earlier.

What Text Is Counted
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Reading duration should count visible article body content.

Do not count:

- HTML tags;
- hidden metadata;
- database-only fields;
- media tokens;
- internal notes;
- moderation notes.

Captions should be counted only when displayed as part of the article body.

AI and Reading Duration
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

If AI is not available, the system formula should compute reading duration.

If AI is available, AI may recommend reading duration, but the system formula should remain available as a fallback and consistency check.

Reading duration is an interface estimate, not a promise that every reader will finish at that exact time. Reading speed varies by person, language, device, complexity, and accessibility method.

Article NSFW Level
------------------

Every completed article output should include an NSFW Level. The level describes the article as a whole; individual media items may receive a different level when their visual treatment is more or less sensitive than the written article.

- None: Safe for general audiences; no meaningful adult, sexual, traumatic, or unusually sensitive content.
- Mild: Non-sexual body exposure, clinical anatomy, ordinary bodily reactions, awkward body topics, or lightly sensitive wellness discussion.
- Sensitive: Sexual-boundary discussions, adult-adjacent terminology, trauma, abuse, serious medical situations, distress, or other material that may require a content notice or limited preview.
- Explicit: Detailed sexualized or graphic adult content. This level should be rare for official Massage Nexus articles and should require deliberate editorial approval.

Men-focused, women-focused, LGBTQ-focused, couples-focused, pregnancy-related, or caregiving content is not automatically NSFW. Classification should be based on the actual treatment of the subject, not the identity of the audience.

Article Rating and Community Response
-------------------------------------

Articles are rated through thumbs up and thumbs down reactions, not through the 1 to 10 rating system.

The 1 to 10 rating system is reserved for experience or product targets such as spas, therapists, and products.

Content reaction targets may include:

- article;
- news;
- guide;
- comic or comic strip;
- review;
- comment;
- discussion thread;
- media upload;
- translation.

For articles, thumbs up/down should answer a simple content question:

- Was this useful, interesting, helpful, clear, or worth reading?

A content reaction rates the content item. It is not a direct rating of the author.

Article reactions may affect the credited author’s reputation through the Reputation System when the reactions create meaningful, weighted, capped, and abuse-checked helpfulness signals. A single article should not be able to create unlimited reputation gain, even when it becomes popular or viral.

For multi-author content, any reputation effect should be shared among credited authors according to the Reputation System rules instead of being duplicated in full for every credited author.

Article reactions should remain separate from article rewards. A user may receive a small reward for reacting, while the article author may receive a small reputation signal only when the reaction passes the Reputation System’s weighting, diminishing-return, and anti-abuse rules.

Article Rewards Reference
-------------------------

Article-related rewards should be defined fully in the User Rewards and Gamification documentation.

Current direction:

- writing an approved article may earn Nexus Points, Embers, Inklings, badges, rank-path progress, or reputation effects depending on author type and policy;
- rating an article with thumbs up/down may earn small rewards, subject to daily caps;
- repeated low-effort voting should not become a farming route;
- AI-generated or AI-assisted portions should not receive the same user-writing credit as fully user-written work unless policy later allows it;
- reputation effects from article reactions should follow the Reputation System, not the reward table.

Working reaction reward reference:

Action                    | Nexus Points | Embers | Inklings
--------------------------+--------------+--------+---------
Thumbs up/down on content | 1            | 10     | 5

A daily reward cap should apply to thumbs up/down actions. Current working cap is 25 rewarded thumbs up/down actions per day. Users may continue reacting after the cap, but they should not receive additional Nexus Points, Embers, or Inklings for those extra reactions on that day.

Article author reputation effects should use weighted helpfulness signals, diminishing returns, per-item caps, and anti-abuse checks from the Reputation System. Article popularity alone should not be treated as proof of high author trust.

This guide does not define public article routes. The article slug remains required metadata, but route structure belongs in the latest Massage Nexus Site Structure Map and related implementation documentation.

its identity exists. article_main.record_note contains Article-wide internal notes;
article_revision.review_note contains the decision about one exact revision, so the fields are not
duplicates.

Workspace Article Editor
------------------------

Article drafting is part of the unified authenticated /workspace experience. My Articles, the
editor, and revision history use the shared workspace navigation; they are not separate global
header destinations or a role-specific sign-in area.

The implemented visual editor uses the open-source Tiptap core with a compact icon toolbar.
Authors may switch between the visual document and its HTML source. Both modes write the same
article_body HTML, and the server-side ArticleContent allowlist remains authoritative when the
draft is saved. Scripts, arbitrary CSS, unsafe links, and unapproved elements or attributes are
removed even when entered in HTML mode.

Current authoring behavior includes:

- title-to-slug generation while the slug remains untouched, with server-side generation as a
  fallback;
- a required short description limited to 255 characters, with a visible character counter;
- headings at levels two through four, paragraphs, emphasis, underline, strikethrough, lists,
  quotations, dividers, approved text alignment, links, undo, redo, and format clearing;
- live word and Unicode character counts, separate visual and spoken reading estimates,
  unsaved-change feedback, and a non-blocking draft readiness summary;
- selection of English, Filipino, Indonesian, Japanese, Portuguese, Spanish, Thai, or Vietnamese as
  the original content language when the Article is created. The original language is locked after
  first save so the Article body and its revisions remain attached to one language identity;
- an ordered author_credit_list that permits registered accounts and custom displayed names.
  author_user_id_list is derived from linked credits for public discovery and reputation, while
  credit alone grants no edit permission;
- article_owner_user_id_list for shared operational ownership. Owners can revise, submit, inspect
  revision history, and unpublish; only the original creator manages the owner list, and the creator
  cannot be removed;
- structured repeated source records with separate title, organization, URL, and publication-
  identifier controls instead of delimiter-encoded text;
- database-backed selectors for related Articles, organizations, establishments, practitioners,
  services, and products. A submitted relationship ID must resolve to an active record;
- article classification, revision notes, comment and sharing preferences in a compact metadata rail;
- anonymous publication preference through article_main.is_anonymous. Public article pages and
  public author listings suppress author_credit_list, while ownership and audit associations remain
  available to authorized reviewers and staff;
- proposed publication scheduling for users with article.schedule. A future date may be recorded,
  but scheduling never bypasses editorial review or publication approval, and the publication job
  remains a separate workflow responsibility.

The current editorial implementation adds an Article review queue at /workspace/editorial/article
for users with editorial workspace access. It shows each Article's latest submitted revision and
opens a safe rendered review page. An editor can approve and publish, request changes with a note,
or reject with a note. A request for changes returns the Article to the contributor's draft view;
approval records the reviewer and approval audit fields and makes the Article public subject to any
future scheduled time.

Function of Neural Agents in Article Work
-----------------------------------------

Neural Agents function as editorial voice lenses, angle generators, draft helpers, reviewers, explainers, and recurring point-of-view systems for Massage Nexus content. They help shape rhythm, tone, examples, humor, skepticism, warmth, seriousness, or instructional clarity while the platform keeps factual responsibility, editorial direction, and publishing control.

A Neural Agent assignment suggests the best-fit voice and angle for a topic; it does not mean the article becomes an uncontrolled character diary or that the platform loses ownership of quality. The article should still feel like a Massage Nexus publication, even when the selected voice is blunt, warm, chaotic, formal, poetic, or humorous.

The canonical Neural Agent writing-style reference is the shared repository JSON file at data/common_reference/neural_agent.json. When creating or assigning an article, use the selected Neural Agent's article_writing_style, tone, humor_style, comment_style, personality, and relevant preferences from that JSON file as the writing-style source. Project-specific copies such as Massage Nexus Neural Agent Profile.json may be used during planning, but the shared common_reference file remains the long-term reusable source.

How to Write a Good Massage Nexus Article
-----------------------------------------

A good Massage Nexus article should begin with a clear reader situation, question, tension, or scene, then guide the reader through the issue in a natural, narrated, well-worded way. It should feel connected to the reader without becoming generic, padded, or artificially simplified. The article should explain what matters, why it matters, what the reader can observe or do, and what practical conclusion they can take away.

The selected Neural Agent's tone may stay intact, whether warm, strict, funny, skeptical, poetic, or blunt, but the article should still be organized and understandable. Personality should give the article flavor; it should not hide the point, confuse the reader, exaggerate claims, or turn useful content into empty performance.

When the article deals with health, safety, pregnancy, medical conditions, senior care, disability, special populations, minors, legal matters, or post-injury situations, it should avoid promising cures or pretending to replace professional medical, legal, or regulatory advice. It may explain general considerations, warning signs, questions to ask, and when to consult a qualified professional.

How to Choose the Right Topic
-----------------------------

Choose topics that help Massage Nexus become useful to real people: first-time spa guidance, massage types, hygiene, safety, communication, pricing, reviews, etiquette, local practice, global spa culture, practitioner professionalism, spa owner operations, wellness habits, products, caregiving, travel, public awareness, and character-friendly fictional what-if features connected to massage or wellness.

A topic is strong when it answers a real reader question, reduces confusion, helps someone avoid a bad experience, explains a misunderstood practice, supports professional standards, or gives Massage Nexus a distinctive point of view. A topic is weak when it is only generic SEO filler, unrelated trivia, or something that could appear on any random website without a Massage Nexus reason for existing.

How to Choose a Proper Article Title
------------------------------------

A proper title should be clear, specific, and honest about what the reader will get. It may be practical, witty, question-based, or editorial, but it should not trick the reader with fake urgency or unrelated clickbait. The best titles usually name the situation, the audience, the problem, or the comparison directly.

Use plain language when the article is for beginners. Use stronger editorial wording when the topic needs a sharper opinion. Use subtitles after a colon when the title needs both a hook and a clear explanation. Sensitive topics should be titled directly and responsibly, not sensationally.

Visual Direction and Media Planning
-----------------------------------

Every full article output should include a concise Visual Direction that can be handed to the separate Massage Nexus article-media thread. It should identify the main visual concept, appropriate image style, preferred aspect ratio when useful, and any important boundaries such as professional draping, no text, no identifiable brand imitation, or no people.

An article may suggest one to five media items. Each item should state its intended placement, what it should show, and its own NSFW Level. Media suggestions should support the article rather than merely decorate it. The separate image-creation guide remains the controlling reference for final image generation and complete media metadata.

Research and Source Use
-----------------------

Massage Nexus articles may use the writing assistant's stable general knowledge when the topic is ordinary, non-current, and primarily explanatory or editorial. However, the assistant should also search for credible resources whenever external research would materially improve accuracy, completeness, cultural context, or usefulness.

Research is especially important for:

- current laws, permits, licensing, registration, taxation, trademarks, and regulatory requirements;
- medical, safety, pregnancy, disability, senior-care, rehabilitation, or contraindication topics;
- country-specific naming practices, language use, cultural expectations, and business customs;
- local pricing, location selection, zoning, transport, demographics, and market conditions;
- historical claims, statistics, scientific claims, product changes, travel guidance, and current industry practice.

Prefer official agencies, primary sources, recognized professional organizations, research publications, and reputable local references. Do not invent sources or cite a source that was not actually consulted.

When sources are used, factual claims that depend on them should be attributed naturally in the article when appropriate, and every consulted source that materially informed the article should appear in the Sources section. Sources may be listed as None used when the article is original commentary, creative writing, general platform guidance, or a stable explanation that genuinely did not require external research.

Completed Article Text-File Output
----------------------------------

When a full article is requested, create a downloadable UTF-8 plain-text file instead of pasting the complete article into the chat. The text file contains all requested metadata, visual planning, article-ready HTML body, reading-duration values, and sources.

If more than one article is requested, create one separate text file for each article.

File Name Rules
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

- Use the final article title as the base file name.
- Preserve normal spaces and readable capitalization.
- Do not convert the file name into a slug and do not replace every space with a dash.
- Add the .txt extension.
- Make the name safe for Windows by removing or replacing \ / : * ? " < > |.
- A colon should normally become  - so the title remains readable.
- Remove trailing periods and spaces.
- Collapse accidental repeated spaces.
- If two requested articles produce the same file name, append  (2),  (3), and so on.

Example:

Code block (text):
    How to Name a Spa Business - A Global Guide.txt

Reusable Prompt Roles
---------------------

Massage Nexus uses three different prompt types:

1. Article Writing Thread Starter Prompt: Used once when opening a dedicated article-writing thread. It establishes editorial scope, categories, audiences, Neural Agents, research behavior, output-file rules, and expected article structure.
2. Follow-Up Article Request Prompt: Used after the thread has been established whenever one or more specific articles should be created.
3. Article Image Thread Starter and Image Request Prompts: Used in a separate media-creation thread and should not be mixed with article drafting.

Naming and Location Article Series
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The spa-naming series should begin with one global guide covering clarity, terminology, brand fit, cultural sensitivity, searchability, and the difference between a business name and its legal registration. Country-specific follow-ups should add local language, cultural expectations, trademark or registration considerations, and current rules without turning every article into registration instructions alone.

The location series should include one general guide about market fit, accessibility, competition, rent, visibility, service model, and customer behavior, plus a separate Metro Manila guide reflecting local transport, density, parking, flooding, building types, neighborhood patterns, and city-specific business conditions.
