DOCUMENT DESCRIPTION

This document defines the fictional visual and story world used by Massage Nexus through The Resting Leaf and the Nexus Cast. It covers the recurring setting, character roles and tiers, continuity, comic direction, comedy standards, visual production standards, language and cultural direction, branded page layout, canonical visual-reference control, anti-drift production rules, working content_comic data direction, topic-planning direction, and the external prompt-file system used for production.

The Nexus Cast are fictional human characters used inside the comic and, when appropriate, as recurring visual models for Massage Nexus social-media posts, advertisements, demonstrations, onboarding scenes, mockups, presentation images, commercials, and other spa- or wellness-related media.

This document is broader than a comic-only guide, but it does not define a final database schema. Collection names, properties, indexes, validations, institution systems, and branded-portal architecture must be finalized in their appropriate technical documentation.

COMIC FEATURE DESCRIPTION

The Resting Leaf is a recurring six-panel slice-of-life comic feature created for Massage Nexus. It is primarily an entertainment and comic-relief feature built around character chemistry, workplace situations, misunderstandings, ordinary life, and expressive visual humor.

Most comics take place inside the fictional Resting Leaf spa, but stories may occasionally move outside the spa when the situation requires it. Possible outside settings include nearby streets, cafés, homes, shops, transport, delivery routes, malls, parks, events, or other places connected to the characters' lives.

The comic may involve massage, spa visits, wellness, work, clients, staff problems, social media, commuting, food, technology, money, relationships, family, neighborhood life, holidays, deliveries, plants, and other ordinary or unusual situations. A strip does not need to teach a massage or wellness lesson to be valid.

Explicit teaching, onboarding, reminders, quizzes, and short educational explanations belong primarily to Massage Campus and the Massage Nexus mascots. The Resting Leaf may still communicate useful information when it arises naturally from the joke, story, or character interaction, but the comic should not read like an instructional pamphlet.

The comic is designed to support Comic of the Day or another frequent publication format. Most strips should be understandable on their own. Occasional multi-part stories, recurring jokes, short arcs, seasonal stories, and continuing character developments are allowed.

CORE STORY DIRECTION

The default story model is a short workplace or slice-of-life situation that reaches a clear comic payoff within six panels.

A normal six-panel rhythm may use:

1. setup;
2. ordinary situation;
3. problem, interruption, or misunderstanding;
4. reaction;
5. escalation;
6. punchline, reversal, or warm comic ending.

This is a flexible rhythm rather than a rigid formula. A strip may use visual comedy, awkward silence, a delayed reaction, a recurring joke, or a nonverbal final panel when that works better.

Most strips should remain standalone. Connected strips may use labels such as Part 1, Part 2, or another story-arc marker in the individual title when readers need to know that the story continues. A visible episode number is not required.

COMEDY STANDARD

The Resting Leaf should feel like a workplace sitcom compressed into six panels, not a spa advertisement with speech balloons. The comic should be entertaining even when the reader learns nothing instructional from it.

Before image generation, identify the main comic mechanism. Useful mechanisms include:

- misunderstanding;
- contradiction;
- escalation;
- reversal;
- delayed realization;
- visual surprise;
- character hypocrisy;
- deadpan response;
- social embarrassment;
- awkward silence;
- ordinary behavior taken too far;
- one character's personality colliding with another character's expectations.

A strip does not need a loud joke in every panel. Setup and reaction panels may be quiet. The final panel should still provide a clear payoff, reversal, revealing expression, or satisfying comic beat.

Characters should not behave like permanently cheerful brand ambassadors. They may look tired, irritated, suspicious, embarrassed, confused, unimpressed, alarmed, bored, awkward, offended, delighted, defeated, or silently judgmental when the story requires it. Attractive characters are allowed to look unprepared or foolish during a joke.

The script should be checked as plain text before the artwork is created. A visually polished page must not be accepted when the joke itself is weak, stiff, confusing, or dependent on a caption explaining why it is funny.

OCCASIONAL RUNNING GAGS

Running gags are optional story devices, not mandatory character behaviors. A character profile may describe a broad humor range, but it must not cause the same joke, reaction, entrance, phrase, or failure to appear automatically.

Specific running-gag ideas should be maintained in a separate topic bank rather than embedded as permanent instructions in this guide. The planning process should avoid repeating the same gag in nearby strips, especially across the first batch of episodes. A recurring joke should return only when the context is fresh, the repetition has a new variation, or the recurrence itself creates a deliberate payoff.

Ordinary one-time situations should remain the default. The comic must not reduce a character to one repeated joke.

CURRENT INFORMATION RULE

When a comic depends on current weather, public events, holidays, schedules, prices, laws, advisories, outages, transport conditions, or other changing information, verify the relevant facts before scripting the episode.

Keep temporary research and event-specific terminology inside that episode's planning notes. Do not expand one weather example, holiday, or current-event discussion into a large permanent rule for the entire fictional world. Use accurate terminology, and do not invent official advisories, closures, safety instructions, or factual claims.

LANGUAGE AND CULTURAL DIRECTION

Primary and Canonical Edition

The primary original and canonical language of The Resting Leaf is natural Metro Manila Taglish.

Dialogue should sound like ordinary conversation among adults in Metro Manila. Characters may switch between English and Tagalog according to personality, emotion, familiarity, social position, work setting, and the situation. Dialogue may use local rhythm, polite particles, casual speech, expressive reactions, and character-specific phrasing.

The dialogue should be composed directly as Taglish. It should not be drafted in English and translated word for word into Tagalog. Formal Filipino should be used only when a character, announcement, document, joke, or deliberate contrast requires it.

Natural Taglish Quality Check

Before publication, read every line aloud and check:

- whether the word order sounds natural;
- whether the amount of English and Tagalog fits the speaker;
- whether po, opo, and other polite forms are used naturally;
- whether the emotional reaction sounds spontaneous rather than written;
- whether the line is short enough for a speech balloon;
- whether the same character would genuinely use that expression;
- whether names, pronouns, and balloon ownership are correct;
- whether the punchline still works without explanation.

Awkward constructions that resemble literal translation, textbook Filipino, or foreign-accented Tagalog should be rewritten. A deliberately unusual way of speaking is allowed only when it belongs to an established character, visitor, formal announcement, or intentional joke.

Future Language Adaptation

The Taglish edition should be produced and stabilized first. English, Spanish, Korean, Cebuano, Japanese, Thai, Chinese, or other versions are future localized editions, not automatic simultaneous translations.

Localized editions should keep:

- the same Nexus Cast;
- the same Resting Leaf identity and surrounding story world;
- the same established character identities and continuity;
- the same series title unless a later branding decision deliberately localizes its presentation.

The broad premise may remain similar, but dialogue, joke timing, honorifics, etiquette, slang, embarrassment, workplace assumptions, holiday references, food, and other cultural details may be rewritten so the comic works naturally for the intended audience. A joke should not be preserved literally when the target culture would not find it natural or funny.

AI may assist with a localized draft, but a fluent native or culturally knowledgeable reviewer should approve it before publication. The user is not expected to judge humor in every language without qualified review.

The proper name The Resting Leaf remains the default international name. A localized subtitle or explanatory label may be considered later without creating a new cast or a separate fictional spa.

A separate cultural_context database property is not required at this stage. Language belongs under language_id. Cultural adaptation remains an editorial and localization responsibility unless later database design establishes a genuine need for additional structured data.

COMIC DRAWING STYLE

Official Style Name

The official production label is:

Modern Slice-of-Life Digital Comic with semi-realistic Western comic linework and warm editorial coloring.

Definition

Modern Slice-of-Life Digital Comic describes the everyday, character-driven storytelling, contemporary webcomic readability, and grounded adult setting.

Semi-realistic Western comic linework means:

- clearly drawn ink contours rather than photographic rendering;
- believable adult anatomy and facial structure;
- simplified but recognizable features;
- confident contour lines and selective interior detail;
- expressive brows, eyes, mouths, hands, posture, and silhouettes;
- no anime facial construction, manga shorthand, chibi proportions, or superhero exaggeration.

Warm editorial coloring means:

- warm, controlled color harmony;
- polished magazine-illustration or graphic-editorial finish;
- soft ambient warmth from wood, skin, lighting, plants, and interior surfaces;
- restrained cel shading combined with subtle painterly texture;
- enough contrast for speech balloons and expressions to remain readable;
- illustrated atmosphere without cinematic photographic bokeh or luxury-advertisement gloss.

The comic must look clearly illustrated rather than like a photograph processed through a weak drawing filter.

Use:

- clean and visible line art;
- warm, controlled colors;
- expressive adult faces;
- readable body language;
- realistic-but-softened proportions;
- lightly exaggerated comic reactions;
- controlled digital shading;
- illustrated rather than photographic textures;
- clear foreground and background separation;
- recognizable character silhouettes;
- movement marks, reaction marks, or simplified effects when useful.

Avoid:

- photorealistic rendering;
- realistic skin pores;
- cinematic photographic bokeh;
- anime;
- manga;
- chibi;
- superhero anatomy;
- children's-cartoon styling;
- generic corporate illustration;
- identical attractive faces;
- excessive luxury-advertisement styling.

The characters should remain adults and should look believable within a Filipino slice-of-life workplace comedy. Expressions and poses may be subtle or exaggerated according to the joke without redesigning the character.

Acting, Expressions, and Poses

Character reference images preserve identity; they do not prescribe the pose or facial expression for every comic appearance. Do not repeatedly copy crossed arms, a peace sign, a presentation smile, or another cast-sheet pose simply because it appears in the reference.

For every panel, define the character's acting state separately. Useful acting choices include:

- deadpan stillness;
- delayed side-eye;
- forced customer-service politeness;
- panic hidden behind a smile;
- visible exhaustion;
- contained irritation;
- suspicious silence;
- embarrassment;
- disbelief;
- dramatic overreaction;
- physical discomfort;
- relief;
- smug satisfaction;
- mischievous anticipation;
- genuine fear;
- quiet amusement.

Not every visible character should smile. Characters should look at the person, object, or event that currently holds their attention rather than posing toward the viewer.

ELASTIC COMIC EXAGGERATION AND SYMBOLIC REACTION EFFECTS

The official style remains Modern Slice-of-Life Digital Comic with semi-realistic Western comic linework and warm editorial coloring. The comic may temporarily depart from normal anatomy, scale, or facial proportion when a joke benefits from clear visual exaggeration.

Permitted temporary comic effects include:

- eyes becoming unusually large during shock, panic, disbelief, pleading, or excitement;
- heart-shaped, star-shaped, spiral, blank, narrowed, or sparkling eyes when the emotion is clear;
- a character's head or body becoming temporarily larger to show anger, authority, excitement, fear, or comic intimidation;
- a character appearing very small to show embarrassment, defeat, guilt, or feeling ignored;
- steam, smoke puffs, storm clouds, flames, sparks, anger marks, impact bursts, sweat drops, hearts, stars, or similar symbolic effects around the head or body;
- speed lines, repeated limbs, dust clouds, stretched poses, motion smears, and exaggerated leaning when someone runs or moves quickly;
- squash-and-stretch poses during slips, jumps, impacts, sudden stops, or physical comedy;
- simplified backgrounds, color bursts, spotlight effects, or symbolic voids during a strong reaction;
- a temporary looming or giant-scale composition when the panel clearly communicates that the effect is emotional or symbolic rather than literal.

These effects are collectively described as elastic comic exaggeration and symbolic reaction effects.

They should be used when they strengthen the joke, not in every panel. A quiet deadpan face may be funnier than an oversized reaction. The amount of exaggeration should depend on the character and situation.

The overall series must not become chibi, anime, manga, or children's-cartoon art. A temporary compact or distorted reaction drawing is allowed inside an otherwise consistent Western digital-comic style, but the character must remain recognizable through hairstyle, clothing, color, facial anchors, and role.

When a panel uses impossible scale or anatomy symbolically, the composition should make that clear through reaction marks, simplified perspective, or a stylized background. The next ordinary panel should return the character to normal proportions unless the joke intentionally continues.

An exploding-head effect means a harmless cartoon burst, smoke cloud, flame, or symbolic anger effect. It must not be rendered as gore, injury, or literal bodily destruction.

STANDARD SIX-PANEL PAGE DESIGN

Page Format

- Aspect Ratio: 3:2 landscape
- Recommended Master Size: 2400 × 1600 pixels
- Acceptable Generation Size When Model Output Is Limited: 1536 × 1024 pixels
- Panel Arrangement: three columns by two rows
- Reading Order: left to right across the upper row, then left to right across the lower row
- Panel Sequence: Panels 1, 2, and 3 on the upper row; Panels 4, 5, and 6 on the lower row
- Panel Shape: rectangular with subtly softened corners
- Outer Page Background: warm off-white
- Panel Border: solid dark charcoal
- Gutters: clean warm off-white spacing
- Default Layout: stable and regular rather than tilted or experimental
- Visible Panel Numbers: 1 through 6, one number per panel

Recommended Measurements at 2400 × 1600 Pixels

- Panel Border Thickness: approximately 5 to 7 pixels
- Panel Corner Radius: approximately 10 to 14 pixels
- Gutter Width: approximately 20 to 28 pixels
- Outer Page Margin: approximately 36 to 48 pixels

These measurements may be proportionally adjusted for other output sizes. The landscape format gives each panel enough horizontal space for character interaction, readable dialogue, background action, and the branded header and footer.

Irregular panels, borderless sequences, inset panels, or special layouts may be used only when the story clearly benefits from them. The normal production format remains three columns by two rows.

Panel Numbering

Standard strips should visibly number the panels from 1 to 6.

Use a small, consistent number box near the upper-left or lower-left corner of each panel. The selected position should remain consistent throughout the page and should not cover faces, dialogue, or important action. Panel numbers help readers follow the intended sequence and make production review easier.

A special experimental layout may omit panel numbers only when explicitly approved.

Dialogue, Lettering, and Logo Fidelity

The six-panel script and exact dialogue must be finalized before image generation. Two production modes are allowed:

1. Pre-lettered generation: the generator attempts to place the approved final dialogue in the correct balloons. This reduces manual work but requires careful checking of spelling, punctuation, Taglish naturalness, balloon order, and speaker ownership.
2. Manual lettering mode: the generator creates empty balloons or clear lettering spaces, and the dialogue is added later in an image editor. This is more reliable when exact text is essential or generated text repeatedly fails.

Pre-lettered generation may be the practical default for ordinary production attempts. Manual correction remains the fallback.

- placeholder words;
- rewritten dialogue not found in the approved script;
- balloons pointing to the wrong speaker;
- captions that invent new facts;

- an object removed from one panel should disappear only after a visible or understandable action;
- wet clothing, towels, puddles, and umbrellas must follow the same time sequence;
- open umbrellas should not normally be used indoors unless the story intentionally explains the behavior;
- an escort scene should place the umbrella outside or at the doorway, not open in the middle of the spa;

For recurring rooms, preserve major visual anchors when visible:

- entrance location;
- reception counter position;
- logo wall or signage;
- major doors and corridor direction;
- waiting chairs;
- treatment bed orientation;
- large windows;
- plant clusters or other recognizable fixtures.

Minor decoration, small objects, plant placement, wall art, and background detail may vary when the change does not confuse the location or interrupt the joke.

Before generation, each panel should have a simple spatial-blocking note identifying where each character stands or sits, what surface they interact with, and which direction they face.

VISUAL REFERENCE AND IDENTITY CONSISTENCY RULES

Existing Canonical Assets

Approved individual character references already exist in several forms, including realistic body references, comic-style references, front and back views, corrected faces, and detailed wardrobe or uniform boards.

Do not require the project to recreate those assets merely to begin a new comic.

The best currently approved version of each visible character controls. A deliberately corrected reference approved by the user takes precedence over older versions and over any later accidental generation.

Reference Saturation and Recency Drift

Do not claim that the image model has a fixed verified memory limit such as four, five, or six references. However, treat the following practical risks as real:

- too many character references competing in one request;
- several versions of the same face;
- realistic and comic references competing without a clear purpose;
- a full-cast sheet competing with individual references;
- detailed wardrobe boards competing with a newly generated incorrect costume;
- later generated images receiving more visual influence merely because they are recent;
- repeated full-page edits gradually redesigning the cast.

More references do not automatically produce more fidelity.

When too many visual instructions compete, the generator may simplify, merge, substitute, average, or ignore details. This can cause faces to converge, uniforms to migrate between characters, supporting cast members to replace core cast members, or corrected errors to reappear.

Canonical Reference Packet

Each comic should use a compact canonical reference packet selected after the visible cast is known.

For each visible character, normally select:

- one approved identity reference;
- one exact wardrobe or uniform reference when the clothing matters;
- one body or build reference only when the silhouette cannot be understood from the identity reference.

Also select:

- one environment reference when the room or location matters;
- one comic-style reference when needed;
- official logo and footer assets.

Do not attach every historical file.

Do not attach several competing versions of the same face or uniform.

Do not attach all eight cast members to a comic that uses only two or three.

Body and Underwear References

Existing underwear or minimally clothed body references may remain useful for preserving body shape and proportions, but they are not required for every ordinary clothed scene.

Use them only when:

- the character's body silhouette has repeatedly drifted;
- the scene requires clothing that reveals or depends on the body form;
- a massage, changing, wet-area, fitness, or other approved scene genuinely needs the anatomical proportion reference.

Do not add them merely because they exist.

Non-Generative Character Contact Sheets

When several separate files for one character are difficult to manage, a single canonical character contact sheet may be assembled manually from the already approved images.

A contact sheet may include:

- face close-up;
- front or three-quarter identity view;
- full-body silhouette;
- uniform front;
- uniform back;
- critical accessories.

This contact sheet should be a non-generative layout using the existing approved pixels. It must not redraw, beautify, average, or reinterpret the character. It can reduce attachment count without spending image-generation credits.

Full-Cast Generation Limitation

A generated image containing all eight named characters has repeatedly caused:

- duplicated faces;
- characters resembling one another;
- names attached to the wrong person;
- missing characters;
- role swaps;
- altered uniforms;
- facial redesign;
- character identity drift.

A full-cast image may exist as an archival or presentation asset.

It is not the authoritative production reference for exact faces, bodies, or uniforms.

The Cast Sheet Generation Prompt remains available only for presentation work. It is deprecated as a method for redefining or preserving canonical identity.

A manually assembled full-cast board made from accepted individual images is preferable when an authoritative lineup is needed.

Authority of Visual References

Use the following visual authority order:

1. current approved individual identity reference;
2. current approved uniform or wardrobe reference;
3. approved body or build reference when necessary;
4. approved environment reference;
5. approved comic-style reference;
6. full-cast presentation sheet;
7. older generated drafts.

When generated output differs from the accepted visual reference:

- the accepted reference controls;
- the written character profile supports the reference;
- the generated variation does not become canon automatically.

Do not interpret an accidental hairstyle, face, costume, body type, skin tone, or role change as a redesign.

Panel Cast Matrix

Before full-page generation, create a panel cast matrix.

For each panel, list:

- characters who must appear;
- established characters who must not appear;
- role and wardrobe for each visible character;
- time and lighting state.

This matrix is mandatory when:

- four or more established characters appear across the page;
- a supporting character appears only in a late panel;
- two characters have similar gender, build, hair, or clothing;
- a decorative header includes cast portraits;
- a previous attempt substituted one character for another.

A character present in a header portrait does not gain permission to appear inside a story panel.

If Javi appears only in Panel 6, Panels 1 through 5 must explicitly exclude Javi.

Visual Lock Matrix

For each visible character, record a compact immutable fingerprint:

- role;
- approved identity reference;
- facial and grooming anchors;
- body silhouette;
- exact wardrobe reference;
- panels in which the character appears;
- panels in which the character is absent.

Do not duplicate the entire biography or fabric specification inside the generation prompt. Use the references for fine detail and list only the features that prevent identity or wardrobe substitution.

Wardrobe Lock

Wardrobe is character-specific.

- Enzo Villaluz uses the accepted male therapist wardrobe reference.
- Iris Montoya uses the accepted female therapist wardrobe reference.
- Caleb Rivera uses scene-appropriate civilian clothing and is not staff.
- Mika Torres uses scene-appropriate civilian clothing and is not staff.
- Gavin Lorico uses the accepted receptionist reference.
- Lara Castillo uses the accepted manager reference.
- Javi Mercado uses the accepted Sugod Express rider reference.
- Rina Salcedo uses earthy plant-vendor casual wear.

A uniform approved for one character must not migrate to another character.

The same character's uniform must remain identical across a continuous scene unless the script shows a change.

Reference Pose Rule

Use references to preserve identity, build, grooming, and wardrobe.

Do not copy the reference pose unless the panel requires it.

Javi does not need to make a peace sign whenever he appears. Lara does not need to cross her arms in every scene. Enzo and Iris must act within the story rather than hold cast-sheet poses.

Generative Edit Drift

A generative image edit is not equivalent to editing layers in Photoshop or another graphics application.

Even when the instruction names one local change, the model may reinterpret:

- untouched faces;
- uniforms;
- panel casting;
- furniture;
- room architecture;
- lighting;
- text;
- decorative details.

Repeated full-page edits may therefore fix one error while creating several new errors.

The latest edited image must not automatically become the new character reference.

Correction Method Priority

Use this order after a page has been generated:

1. non-generative manual correction;
2. one isolated targeted generative correction;
3. clean full-page regeneration from the canonical packet.

Prefer manual or deterministic correction for:

- dialogue lettering;
- logo replacement;
- footer replacement;
- clock faces or digital times;
- small dots, lines, borders, and decorative marks;
- color grading;
- crop and alignment;
- panel numbers;
- signs and labels when editable.

Use a targeted generative correction only when the defect is isolated and does not require the model to reinterpret several characters.

Do not chain repeated broad full-page generative edits.

As a project default, allow no more than one targeted generative correction from the first page. If another broad correction is needed, perform a clean reset unless the user explicitly chooses otherwise.

Clean Reset Rule

A clean reset means:

- retain the approved script and planning text;
- return to the original approved character and wardrobe references;
- exclude drifted generations from character authority;
- use the latest approved panel cast and visual lock matrices;
- generate a fresh full page rather than editing the distorted page again.

The user prefers a complete first-pass page rather than routine panel-by-panel generation. Panel-by-panel generation remains an optional fallback, not the default production method.

CHARACTER CANON AND CONTINUITY

The characters have lives beyond the physical spa. A comic may establish new information about:

- pets;
- relatives;
- friendships;
- relationships;
- previous employment;
- education;
- hobbies;
- food preferences;
- recurring possessions;
- personal habits;
- homes;
- neighborhoods;
- transportation;
- past events;
- ongoing problems;
- temporary injuries or situations.

A fact revealed in a published comic becomes part of continuity when it is presented as true rather than as a joke, lie, dream, misunderstanding, rumor, or uncertain claim.

Newly established facts must be preserved so that pets, relatives, relationships, preferences, possessions, or past events do not disappear or contradict future stories without explanation.

Stable Profile and Published Canon

The cast reference should separate:

- stable profile information established before publication;
- published canon information first established in a released comic;
- temporary continuity information that lasts only for a limited story;
- uncertain or disputed information that a character claimed but the story did not confirm.

Cast Reference Storage

A maintained JSON reference file is sufficient for the Nexus Cast at the current project stage. The working filename is:

nexus_cast.json

The file may contain stable character keys used by comic production and by cast_key_list in content_comic. These keys are controlled internal references stored in the project JSON file. They are not yet a database taxonomy and not database record identifiers.

A dedicated database collection for the Nexus Cast is not currently required because the cast is small, fixed, editorially controlled, and rarely changed.

A future cast collection may be reconsidered if the website later needs public character profile pages, searchable character records, automated character timelines, relationship browsing, cast administration, or other database-driven features. If that happens, cast_key_list may later be replaced or supplemented by cast_id_list.

Story Bible Meaning and Current Decision

A story bible is a continuity reference containing the fictional world's rules, recurring locations, character relationships, established facts, running jokes, story arcs, and details that later stories should not contradict.

A separate story-bible file is not required now. This document together with nexus_cast.json already performs that function. A separate file should be created only if the amount of story continuity becomes too large or complicated for the current references.

COMIC PRODUCTION WORKFLOW

Production Modes

The process must remain practical for one person managing articles, research, listings, reviews, outreach, development, and other Massage Nexus work.

Two production modes are allowed:

1. Quick Production Mode

The user gives a topic, situation, or rough idea. The assistant performs the story, comedy, Taglish, cast, continuity, reference, and generation-risk checks internally.

When the user asks for creation in the same request, the assistant may proceed after those checks without forcing the user to review a long planning form.

2. Review-First Mode

The assistant presents the six-panel plan, dialogue, panel cast matrix, visual lock matrix, continuity lock, and compact reference packet before generation.

Use this when:

- the user explicitly asks to plan first;
- the comic introduces major canon;
- the joke is sensitive;
- exact timing or spatial continuity is important;
- four or more characters are used;
- previous attempts have suffered identity or wardrobe drift.

The detailed planning template is a production tool, not a mandatory burden for every strip.

Preferred Generation Strategy

The user's preferred default is one complete six-panel page in the first approved generation.

Do not automatically require six separately generated panels.

Support a strong full-page first pass by:

- using the smallest cast that preserves the joke;
- selecting only necessary references;
- locking panel occupancy;
- locking wardrobe by character;
- simplifying risky body and furniture interactions;
- using readable exact-time displays;
- removing unnecessary header decoration;
- identifying high-risk details before generation.

If the first-pass plan is too complex, simplify the plan before spending image credits rather than relying on many corrective generations afterward.

1. Verify Current Information When Needed

When an episode depends on changing facts, verify only the facts needed for that story and keep temporary research in the episode plan.

2. Select the Story and Comic Mechanism

Choose the situation, mechanism, required cast, setting, and final payoff.

Confirm that the joke works as plain text before art generation.

3. Limit and Assign the Cast

Use one to three characters when practical.

Add more only when the joke genuinely requires them.

Create a panel cast matrix that lists required and forbidden established characters in every panel.

4. Check Character and Story Continuity

Review stable profiles, published canon, connected stories, and temporary continuity.

Distinguish deliberate role exploration from accidental role swapping.

5. Write and Test the Six-Panel Script

For each panel, define:

- visible characters;
- spatial blocking;
- gaze;
- action;
- expression;
- dialogue;
- balloon ownership;
- time and lighting;
- important props;
- continuity change.

Confirm that setup, escalation, and payoff are visually understandable.

6. Perform the Natural Taglish Check

Read every line aloud.

Rewrite stiff, translated, overly formal, or unnatural phrasing.

Keep dialogue short enough for the panel and appropriate to the speaker.

7. Lock Visual Continuity

Lock:

- character identity;
- wardrobe;
- hairstyle and facial hair;
- relative height;
- props;
- exact time;
- weather;
- wet or dry state;
- room anchors;
- furniture and floor levels;
- object positions;
- temporary injuries or conditions.

8. Prepare Spatial Blocking

State where each character stands, sits, lies, enters, exits, and looks.

Identify any risk of a body passing through a table, counter, chair, floor, wall, door, or another person.

9. Build the Visual Lock Matrix

For each visible character, record the compact identity and wardrobe fingerprint needed for generation.

Do not copy full biographies into the image prompt.

10. Select the Compact Canonical Reference Packet

Use only the strongest current references for the visible scene.

Do not attach all eight cast references automatically.

Do not attach several conflicting versions of the same face or uniform.

A full-cast sheet is secondary and presentation-only.

11. Choose the Lettering Mode

Choose:

- pre-lettered generation using the approved exact text; or
- editable balloons and manual lettering.

Pre-lettered generation may remain the practical default when dialogue is short. Manual correction remains the reliability fallback.

12. Perform the First-Pass Generation Preflight

Before generation, confirm:

- every panel has an approved cast list;
- every visible character has one identity reference;
- every work-role character has the correct uniform reference;
- no unrelated reference is attached;
- exact times are readable;
- blocking is physically possible;
- header and footer contain no invented text;
- final panel visibly supports the payoff.

13. Generate One Complete Page

Generate the complete three-column by two-row page from the approved packet.

Do not use a drifted previous page as character authority.

14. Review Identity, Uniform, Role, and Geometry

Check:

- faces;
- body types;
- skin tones;
- hairstyles;
- facial hair;
- character substitutions;
- uniforms;
- roles;
- panel occupancy;
- furniture intersections;
- floor and counter levels;
- exact times;
- room anchors;
- props;
- expressions.

15. Review Dialogue and Branding

Check:

- exact Taglish;
- spelling and punctuation;
- speaker ownership;
- balloon tails;
- title;
- Resting Leaf branding;
- Massage Nexus footer;
- absence of social-media information and invented slogans.

16. Choose the Correct Correction Method

Use manual correction for deterministic graphic details.

Use one targeted generative correction only for an isolated visual defect.

If the page has several identity, uniform, role, or architecture errors, stop the edit chain and perform a clean reset.

17. Prepare Publication Metadata

Prepare comic_title, comic_slug, short_description, language_id, alternative_text, caption, comic_transcript when retained, tags, NSFW level, cast references, status, and scheduling or publication timestamp.

18. Preserve New Canon

Update nexus_cast.json only when a published strip deliberately establishes a new permanent, temporary, or uncertain fact.

Accidental generated details never become canon.

- a decorative header portrait did not cause a character to enter an unauthorized panel;
- every staff or rider character wears the exact approved wardrobe in every relevant panel;
- no client, receptionist, manager, rider, or vendor was silently reassigned to another role;
- story-critical clock or time displays show the approved time clearly;
- the page was not chained from a drifted generation as character authority;
- the title is correct;
- no visible episode number is present;
- no visible publication date is present;
- the branded Massage Nexus footer follows the approved standard layout;
- no social-media handles, icons, Follow Us block, or QR code appears in the footer;
- the copyright line reads © 2026. MassageNexus.com;
- distorted official logos or wordmarks have been corrected manually;
- the dialogue sounds natural when read aloud in Metro Manila Taglish;
- the dialogue is not a literal or awkward translation;
- the joke has a clear comic mechanism and payoff;
- the comic does not read like forced instruction or a promotional brochure;
- all visible characters match their accepted references;
- every character remains in the established role unless a deliberate and clearly explained storyline temporarily explores another activity;
- deliberate learning, assistance, role-play, dream, or costume situations are visually explained rather than appearing as accidental role swaps;
- no unapproved therapist, receptionist, manager, or other recurring staff character appears;
- the core spa cast, frequent clients, and supporting cast are used according to the story rather than forced into every strip;
- no romantic pairing or implied shipping is introduced among the established Nexus Cast;
- background guests remain secondary and non-recurring unless intentionally designed;
- no two characters have collapsed into the same face;
- reference poses have not been copied mechanically;
- expressions suit the moment and the cast is not smiling without reason;
- wardrobe remains consistent through continuous scenes;
- towels, draping, underwear, face cradles, and massage props remain consistent;
- umbrellas and wet-weather props behave logically;
- no umbrella, mop, plant, limb, or furniture passes through the floor, wall, counter, or another object;
- character scale, floor level, counter height, and furniture geometry remain plausible;
- room anchors remain recognizable across panels;
- minor background changes do not confuse the location;
- speech balloons point to the correct speakers;
- generated placeholder text has been removed;
- current-event terminology has been verified when relevant;
- the setting supports the story without unnecessarily emphasizing luxury;
- alternative text describes the visual sequence;
- newly established character facts have been identified for continuity;
- accidental generation details have not been added to canon.

SAMPLE GENERATION REVIEW AND PRODUCTION LESSONS

The early sample strips and Grand Opening generations are production tests unless a final page is explicitly approved for publication.

The Grand Opening story currently has an approved title, script, joke, cast, dialogue, and continuity plan. Its final artwork is not yet approved.

Useful directions observed:

- the three-column by two-row landscape format works;
- visible panel numbers improve reading order and review;
- the branded header and Massage Nexus footer can provide a strong identity;
- pre-lettered dialogue may be usable when short and carefully checked;
- varied expressions create a more convincing workplace comedy;
- explicit clock targets can be improved when they are large and clearly requested;
- an isolated geometry defect may sometimes be repaired.

Grand Opening production failures revealed a more important limitation:

- repairing one area through a full-page generative edit may alter unrelated areas;
- Enzo may be replaced by Javi in a panel where Javi does not belong;
- Gavin's already accepted receptionist uniform may be redesigned even when no change was requested;
- Lara's wardrobe may migrate toward another role;
- Enzo's uniform may be corrected in some panels but not others;
- faces may drift farther from the approved cast after repeated generations;
- a hallucinated body-and-table intersection may be fixed while another identity or wardrobe error appears;
- later edits may treat a previous generation's mistake as the new visual pattern.

The problem is not solved by adding every available reference to every request. Too many references and competing versions can increase confusion.

The production response is:

- plan a strong first pass;
- use a compact canonical reference packet;
- lock cast per panel;
- lock wardrobe by character;
- keep decorative header portraits optional;
- prefer manual deterministic correction;
- avoid repeated full-page generative editing;
- perform a clean reset when identity drift appears.

Problems that must not be repeated:

- visible episode numbers or publication dates;
- invented header subtitles or promotional slogans;
- social-media information in the standard footer;
- distorted official logos treated as final;
- clients or outside characters being changed into staff;
- one established character replacing another;
- an unnamed generated therapist being treated as established staff;
- props disappearing;
- clothing, underwear, towels, or equipment changing without cause;
- cast-sheet poses copied mechanically;
- constant smiling;
- impossible floor, counter, table, chair, or body geometry;
- room changes that make one continuous location look unrelated;
- dialogue that sounds mechanically translated;
- a weak or unexplained payoff.

None of the accidental faces, role changes, uniforms, unidentified characters, generated dialogue, or events from failed generations become canon.

WORKING MEDIA AND DATA DIRECTION

Current Collection Decision

The current working collection name is:

content_comic

This replaces the earlier tentative comic_main direction. It is singular, uses snake_case, and may be renamed later if a broader content architecture creates a better reason.

The following are not required:

- series_key;
- comic_key;
- content_style;
- comic_format;
- thumbnail_media_image_id;
- a separate comic_panel collection.

The Resting Leaf is currently the only comic series, so series_key adds no useful distinction. comic_title and comic_slug provide the content title and public slug. conversational Taglish is the normal editorial direction rather than a variable content_style value. The format is currently the standard six-panel comic, so comic_format should be added only if the platform later supports meaningfully different comic formats.

Property Notes

comic_title

The individual title of the comic strip. The Resting Leaf remains the visible series title and does not need to be repeated as series_key.

comic_slug

The public URL-safe slug used by the comic detail route.

short_description

A concise description used for previews, cards, search results, or sharing.

language_id

References the language used by the published comic version. The exact language-reference implementation will be discussed separately.

cast_key_list

Stores the stable keys of recurring Nexus Cast members appearing in the strip. The keys currently come from nexus_cast.json. This is a controlled project reference, not yet a database taxonomy. Generic unnamed background extras do not need cast keys.

alternative_text

Describes the meaningful visual sequence for accessibility. It should not merely repeat the caption.

caption

A concise public caption or supporting text shown with the comic when needed.

comic_transcript

Optional readable text containing the final dialogue and relevant captions. It supports accessibility, search, correction, and future language adaptation without creating separate panel records. A detailed panel-by-panel production script is not required after publication.

tag_list

Working tag values for discovery. Final tag storage and controlled-value behavior should follow the project database design.

level_nsfw

The content-sensitivity level for the complete comic.

status_publication

The publication lifecycle state, such as draft, scheduled, published, archived, or another later approved status.

scheduled_at

Used only while the comic is scheduled for future release. It records the intended publication moment.

When the comic is actually published:

- write the actual publication moment to published_at;
- remove or unset scheduled_at from the MongoDB document rather than keeping an obsolete scheduled value;
- retain created_at because the creation moment remains historically different from scheduling and publication.

published_at

Records the actual publication moment. It may differ from created_at and from the former scheduled_at value.

created_at

Records when the comic record was created.

updated_at

Records the most recent meaningful update to the record.

record_note

Stores internal notes according to the wider project record-note direction.

Timestamp Direction

Exact moments use the *_at naming direction from Shared Project Standards v2.60. Timestamps should be stored unambiguously, normally in UTC, and displayed in the relevant user or publication time zone.

No Separate Panel Collection

comic_panel is removed from the current direction.

Panel information means the planning material for each panel: visual action, expression, dialogue, balloon placement, and continuity. This information is useful during production but does not need six separate database documents.

The default post-publication direction is:

- retain the finished comic image;
- retain the final dialogue through comic_transcript when useful;
- retain alternative_text and caption;
- do not retain a mandatory structured panel script in the database;
- keep detailed planning notes only in the working chat or source file when they remain useful.

Image Storage and Media Relationship

The comic is always published as an image-based work, but the binary image should still be stored in Azure Blob Storage rather than inside the MongoDB document.

The final relationship between content_comic and media_image remains under discussion.

Current architectural recommendation:

- content_comic stores editorial and publication information;
- media_image stores technical file information, Azure Blob location, dimensions, format, file size, processing history, and thumbnail or derivative handling;
- content_comic references one media_image_id;
- do not add thumbnail_media_image_id because the media system already handles thumbnail information;
- alternative_text, caption, and comic_transcript may remain in content_comic because they describe the published comic context.

A one-to-one relationship does not by itself make media_image unnecessary. Embedding all image properties directly in content_comic would duplicate the media system and make later processing, replacement, rights, derivatives, and storage management less consistent.

This recommendation is not yet a final user decision. If content_comic later owns the technical image properties directly, the required storage, format, dimension, file-size, derivative, and thumbnail fields must be defined explicitly.

Current Master Export Direction

- Master aspect ratio: 3:2 landscape
- Recommended master dimensions: 2400 × 1600 pixels
- Acceptable generation dimensions when model output is limited: 1536 × 1024 pixels
- Master file format: PNG
- Recommended web derivative: WebP generated from the approved PNG master
- Thumbnail handling: through the selected media system rather than a duplicate content_comic thumbnail field

The PNG master should be retained for quality and later manual logo, footer, dialogue, or lettering correction.
