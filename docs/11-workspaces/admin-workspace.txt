The Admin Workspace is part of /workspace and is shown only through permissions. It should not rely on an unprotected /admin shortcut. It brings together data administration, moderation, verification, editorial management, user access, taxonomy, media, integrations, system operations, analytics, audit, and support.

Administrative capability is not a user-selected workspace mode. The unified workspace automatically
adds the permitted administrative navigation and dashboard areas alongside the user's ordinary and
other assigned capabilities. There is no admin-role dropdown or context switcher. Every panel and
record action still enforces authorization on the server.

This document defines administrative capabilities, workflow boundaries, states, decisions, and safeguards. Screen layout, components, responsive behavior, and detailed interaction patterns are defined in Massage Nexus Admin Workspace User Interface.

Administrator does not mean that every administrator receives every permission.

================================================================================
1. PRIMARY GOALS
================================================================================

The Admin Workspace should:

- provide safe structured control of platform operations;
- separate high-risk actions from ordinary editing;
- give each administrator only necessary access;
- preserve audit history;
- manage queues and exceptions;
- support bulk work without enabling accidental mass damage;
- avoid direct uncontrolled database editing;
- support editors, moderators, verifiers, support staff, and technical administrators;
- provide clear system health and operational status;
- keep public editorial trust separate from advertising influence.

2. ACCESS MODEL
================================================================================

Possible administrative participants:

Founder
Highest project decision authority. This does not create an unaudited technical bypass or exempt the Founder from high-risk safeguards.

Platform Administrator
Broad platform management.

Data Administrator
Structured record and taxonomy management.

Editorial Administrator
Articles, Service content, help, and publication.

Moderation Administrator
Reports, abuse, appeals, and content enforcement.

Verification Administrator
Claims, credentials, documents, and trust signals.

User Access Administrator
Accounts, roles, permissions, and security support.

System Administrator
Jobs, storage, integrations, configuration, backup, and maintenance.

Advertising Administrator
Campaign, creative, placement, invoice, and sponsor review.

Support Administrator
Tickets, user assistance, escalation, and account recovery.

A person may have several permission groups. Access should be explicit.

2.1 Case-Scoped Participants

Two participants referenced elsewhere in this document (see section 3) are not Admin Workspace
administrator roles and are intentionally lighter-weight than the participants listed above:

Steward
Defined authoritatively in docs/02-governance/edit-system.txt. May maintain assigned unclaimed
or community-managed records; authority is limited to assigned record types or areas rather than
a broad permission group.

Translator
A person assigned to translate specific content or interface strings within their assigned
language and content scope, per docs/13-localization/translation-system.txt. Translator access
does not by itself grant Editorial Administrator publication authority; translated content still
requires the applicable editorial or interface approval before publication (see section 15 and
section 16).

Both participants receive Standard Security Panel access scoped to their assigned records or
language rather than general Editorial Administrator access.

3. ROUTE DIRECTION
================================================================================

Administrative areas remain under:

/workspace

Administrative routes use permission-scoped panels. Route separation alone does not isolate authentication. Where risk requires stronger isolation, panels use separately configured authentication contexts, cookie names, guards, session rotation, idle and absolute lifetimes, step-up authentication, and middleware enforcement.

- Standard Security Panel (/workspace/editorial, /workspace/advertising, /workspace/analytics)
  Access for Editorial Administrator and Advertising Administrator, plus the case-scoped Translator and Steward participants (see section 2). Requires verified email.
- Medium Security Panel (/workspace/moderation, /workspace/verification, /workspace/support, /workspace/database)
  Access for Moderation Administrator, Verification Administrator, Support Administrator, and Data Administrator. Media previews are redacted by default.
- Critical Security Panel (/workspace/system, /workspace/api, /workspace/audit)
  Access for Founder, System Administrator, and User Access Administrator. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA), recent step-up authentication, shorter session limits, and stronger device or network controls are enforced. IP allowlisting may be used where operationally supportable, but it is not the only protection and requires an audited recovery path. /workspace/api is included here because it manages credentials, scopes, and secret rotation; /workspace/audit is included because it is the read-only viewer for append-only audit events (see section 29).

Platform Administrator is a broad role rather than a single-panel role: per section 37 ("Permissions are more important than broad role names"), a Platform Administrator's actual panel access is determined by which specific permissions are granted, which may span Standard, Medium, and Critical panels at once. Do not treat holding the Platform Administrator title as automatic access to every panel.

Raw technical tools within /workspace/database (see section 6) require Critical-tier controls even though ordinary structured editing in that route is Medium-tier.

4.1 Unified Administrative Work Queue
-------------------------------------

The Admin Workspace uses one permission-filtered queue framework for work requiring human review. It does not combine all records into one collection or allow every administrator to see every queue.

Queue categories may include:

- new spa or establishment submission;
- spa or establishment correction;
- new therapist submission;
- therapist correction;
- Product, Seller, and Service submission;
- taxonomy suggestion;
- pending or reported Review;
- reported Comment, Discussion, Message, or media;
- Article and News editorial review;
- translation review;
- profile claim;
- credential and verification review;
- user report and appeal;
- Support escalation;
- advertising approval;
- data-quality and provenance exception;
- failed import, integration, or automated job;
- high-risk action awaiting second approval.

Every queue item should expose only information allowed by the administrator's effective permission and case scope. A queue item may contain:

- queue and case identifier;
- item type and target record;
- submitter or system source;
- submission date and last activity;
- priority, risk, and privacy class;
- status;
- assignment and assignment expiry;
- due date or response target;
- current and proposed values;
- evidence and provenance;
- duplicate or related-record candidates;
- policy and validation warnings;
- prior decisions and appeals;
- public preview where applicable.

Common queue actions may include:

- assign or release;
- approve;
- approve selected fields;
- reject;
- request changes or more evidence;
- merge or link duplicate work;
- defer;
- escalate;
- transfer to another queue;
- preserve evidence;
- add an internal note;
- notify the submitter;
- reopen or begin an appeal.

Queue processing must prevent silent simultaneous decisions. Assignment leases, optimistic locking, or another approved concurrency control should detect when another administrator has changed or completed the item.

Each domain specification remains authoritative for what may be approved. The common queue framework owns assignment, status, evidence presentation, decision recording, notification, escalation, and audit behavior.

10.1 Access Governance
----------------------

Administrative authorization is deny by default.

Requirements:

- no shared administrator account;
- no self-granting of privileged access;
- no self-approval where a second approver is required;
- separation of ordinary editing, moderation, verification, finance, user access, and system operations;
- temporary or just-in-time access where permanent privilege is unnecessary;
- effective and expiry dates for temporary assignments;
- reason and approver for every privileged assignment;
- periodic access review;
- immediate offboarding and session revocation when authority ends;
- recusal for conflicts of interest;
- separate treatment of human administrators, service accounts, and automated jobs;
- an audited, time-limited break-glass process for emergencies.

Privilege changes rotate or revoke affected sessions as required. The system should notify the affected administrator and security owner of high-risk grants, revocations, and unusual use.

Effective permission is calculated from account status, Massage Nexus membership status, role, direct permission, context, record relationship, time limit, security state, and any active restriction. Hiding navigation is not authorization.

11. VIEW-AS AND SUPPORT ACCESS
================================================================================

A limited View-As feature may help support staff understand a user's interface.

Rules:

- no password access;
- strictly Read-Only session (impersonators must never execute write queries, bookings, or password updates on behalf of the user);
- visible banner;
- no hidden impersonation;
- action restrictions;
- reason required;
- time limit;
- audit entry;
- user notification by default, with a recorded security or legal exception when notice must be delayed or omitted;
- no access to highly sensitive records unless separately authorized.

View-As should show permission results without creating an ordinary reusable user session.

Read-only behavior must be enforced by the authorization and data-access layers, not only by disabled interface controls. View-As must deny state-changing requests, bookings, Messages, exports, password and MFA changes, payout changes, API credential creation, and protected downloads outside the approved support-case scope.

Starting, extending, and ending View-As should be visible to the support worker. The session must end automatically at expiry, privilege loss, account-security change, or explicit termination.

19.1 Third-Party Service Operations
-----------------------------------

The workspace must separately manage services consumed by Massage Nexus. Possible service categories include:

- AI model and embedding providers;
- maps, places, geocoding, directions, and distance services;
- email;
- SMS;
- push notification;
- payment and payout;
- identity or credential verification;
- search;
- object storage and CDN;
- image and video processing;
- translation;
- analytics;
- monitoring and error reporting.

For each configured provider or service, authorized administrators should be able to see:

- service and provider;
- environment;
- operational status;
- enabled capabilities, models, or endpoints;
- request, success, failure, and retry counts;
- quota used, quota remaining, and reset period;
- rate-limit status;
- token, character, message, storage, bandwidth, or other billable usage unit;
- estimated cost, approved budget, and forecast;
- cost by capability, application, market, organization, or approved internal dimension;
- latency and error rate;
- credential age and rotation status without revealing the secret;
- recent incidents;
- fallback or degraded-mode availability;
- last successful health check.

Controls may include:

- warning threshold;
- hard usage or spending limit where supported;
- per-capability, per-user, per-organization, or per-market quota;
- approved model or endpoint restriction;
- emergency disable;
- fallback provider;
- approved model downgrade;
- retry and circuit-breaker control;
- alert routing;
- maintenance note.

Provider controls must not expose credentials, raw private prompts, complete private responses, payment secrets, or unnecessary personal data. Usage records follow privacy, security, retention, and audit rules.

AI usage should distinguish input, cached input, output, image, audio, tool, embedding, and other billable units supported by the provider. Maps usage should distinguish map loads, places search, details, geocoding, routes, distance, and other enabled billable endpoints. Cost reporting must not assume every provider uses the same quota model.

Credential rotation, service disablement, quota changes, provider switching, and budget-limit changes are high-risk actions when they can interrupt production or change data processing.

Theme administration should support permission-scoped editing of theme definitions, asset and
translation completeness checks, accessible preview, validation, draft and published states,
activation scheduling where supported, version history, rollback, and audit. A theme must not be
published when required assets, tokens, contrast checks, or compatibility validation fail.

Cache operations should provide targeted invalidation by environment, capability, target, locale,
permission scope, key family, or revision. Global cache clearing is a high-risk fallback rather
than the ordinary action. The interface must distinguish authoritative-data changes from cache
invalidation and report partial failures.

24. MAINTENANCE AND LOCK MODES
================================================================================

Possible modes:

Normal
Platform operates normally.

Read Only
Public reading continues, but selected changes are blocked.

Maintenance
Most public access is temporarily unavailable.

Registration Lock
New registration is blocked.

Booking Lock
Booking changes are blocked.

Upload Lock
New uploads are blocked.

Emergency Security Lock
Selected sensitive operations stop immediately.

The system should show an appropriate public and workspace message.

Because the system operates on horizontally scaled multi-instance environments, lock state and maintenance modes must be persisted in a shared MongoDB lock collection or distributed cache (like Redis), rather than a local file-based lock.

Audit entries should be strictly append-only. Audit writing and audit viewing use separate access principals: an append-only writer may create events but cannot update, delete, or browse them, while an authorized read-only viewer may search and export permitted events but cannot alter them. Ordinary application, Founder, and administrator sessions do not receive update or delete access to audit events.

The audit system should add tamper-evidence or immutable retention where risk requires it. Time synchronization, correlation identifiers, redaction, retention, backup, export protection, and access auditing apply. Operational logs, security events, record revision history, case history, and immutable audit events remain distinct even when they are linked.

30. HIGH-RISK ACTIONS
================================================================================

Examples:

- permanent deletion;
- restore backup;
- change global permission;
- issue administrator role;
- expose private media;
- approve disputed ownership;
- revoke verification;
- suspend high-profile account;
- edit legal policy;
- change payment destination;
- rotate production secret;
- run destructive bulk action.

Possible safeguards:

- recent authentication (step-up TOTP/password check immediately prior to execution);
- dual-control/two-person approval rules for highly critical actions (like raw database dumps, credential promotions, or secret rotation);
- explicit confirmation;
- typed reason;
- second approver;
- delayed execution;
- notification;
- audit;
- rollback where possible.

When two-person approval is required, the requester cannot approve their own action and the approver must hold the required permission independently. Approval expires after the defined period or when the proposed action changes. Execution must revalidate permission, current state, and approval immediately before applying the action.

- screen-reader-safe status and validation messages;
- no hover-only action or disclosure;
- contrast and non-color status indicators;
- reduced-motion behavior;
- accessible charts and text alternatives;
- captions, transcripts, and safe media warnings where relevant;
- preserved focus after modal, queue, and bulk actions;
- confirmation near the risky action;
- locale-aware date, time, number, currency, address, and time-zone display;

Reviewers exposed to disturbing, abusive, or explicit material should have safe previews, deliberate reveal, exposure preferences, rotation or workload controls where practical, and access to escalation procedures.

34. INCIDENT RESPONSE
================================================================================

The Admin Workspace should support, but not replace, the approved incident-response runbook.

Minimum incident workflow:

1. detect or receive report;
2. create incident and assign severity;
3. assign incident commander or responsible owner;
4. preserve relevant evidence and correlation identifiers;
5. contain affected access, credential, integration, content, or operation;
6. assess privacy, security, legal, payment, safety, and availability impact;
7. notify approved internal and external parties;
8. recover and validate service;
9. monitor for recurrence;
10. complete post-incident review and tracked corrective actions.

Incident controls may revoke sessions, disable integrations, rotate credentials, restrict media, activate a lock mode, pause jobs, or preserve legal holds according to permission. The interface must not expose sensitive incident details to unauthorized administrators.

37. CURRENT WORKING DECISIONS
================================================================================

- Administration remains inside /workspace.
- No unprotected /admin shortcut is required.
- Permissions are more important than broad role names.
- High-risk actions receive stronger safeguards.
- Structured forms are preferred over raw database editing.
- Audit history is required.
- Advertising cannot control organic editorial or trust systems.
- AI assists but does not make final high-impact decisions.
- Private documents and media remain permission-protected.
- Large administration areas are split into focused modules.
- The Admin Workspace contains operational queues and decisions, not only login and access configuration.
- Approval, verification, claim authority, publication, and permission grant remain separate decisions.
- One common queue framework supports domain-specific workflows without replacing their authoritative rules.
- Third-party API, AI, maps, communication, storage, search, and processing usage require health, quota, budget, and cost visibility.
- Administrative UI behavior is defined separately in Massage Nexus Admin Workspace User Interface.
- Theme publication, Quotes, Surveys, Quizzes, Announcements, targeted cache invalidation, and
  aggregate recomputation are permission-scoped administrative capabilities.
